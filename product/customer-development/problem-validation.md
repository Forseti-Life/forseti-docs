# Problem Validation

**Last Updated**: 2024-12-13  
**Status**: 🟡 Not Started

---

## Overview

Problem validation is the first step in Customer Development. The goal is to validate that:
1. **The problem exists** (customers actually have this problem)
2. **The problem is painful** (they care enough to do something about it)
3. **They're actively looking** (not just complaining, but seeking solutions)

**Key Principle**: Fall in love with the problem, not the solution.

---

## Problem Hypothesis

### Primary Problem Statement

**We believe that** _[customer segment]_ **experiences** _[problem]_ **when** _[situation]_.

Example:
> We believe that urban commuters experience anxiety about personal safety when walking through unfamiliar neighborhoods, especially after dark.

### Why This Problem Matters

**Impact on customers**:
- Emotional: _[e.g., Fear, anxiety, stress]_
- Financial: _[e.g., Taking expensive Uber instead of walking]_
- Time: _[e.g., Taking longer "safe" routes]_
- Social: _[e.g., Avoiding social events in certain areas]_

### Current Alternatives

**How do customers solve this problem today?**

1. **Alternative 1**: _[e.g., Avoid certain areas entirely]_
   - Cost: _[Lost opportunities, limited mobility]_
   - Satisfaction: _[1-10 rating]_

2. **Alternative 2**: _[e.g., Check city crime statistics websites]_
   - Cost: _[Time, inaccurate/outdated data]_
   - Satisfaction: _[1-10 rating]_

3. **Alternative 3**: _[e.g., Ask friends/use word of mouth]_
   - Cost: _[Anecdotal, inconsistent, biased]_
   - Satisfaction: _[1-10 rating]_

4. **Alternative 4**: _[e.g., Do nothing, accept risk]_
   - Cost: _[Anxiety, potential danger]_
   - Satisfaction: _[1-10 rating]_

## Customer Discovery Interview Script

### Introduction (2 minutes)

> "Hi [Name], thanks for taking the time to chat with me today. I'm researching how people think about personal safety when they're out and about in the city. I'm not selling anything - I'm just trying to learn about your experiences and challenges.
>
> This should take about 15-20 minutes. I'll ask you some questions about your daily routines and safety concerns. There are no right or wrong answers - I'm just interested in your honest perspective.
>
> Is it okay if I take notes during our conversation? Everything you share will be kept confidential."

### Background Questions (3 minutes)

_Goal: Understand their context and build rapport._

1. "Tell me a bit about yourself - where do you live and what do you do?"
2. "How long have you lived in [city/neighborhood]?"
3. "What's your typical daily routine like?"
4. "Do you usually walk, drive, bike, or take public transit?"

### Problem Discovery Questions (10 minutes)

_Goal: Uncover if they have the problem and how painful it is._

**⚠️ Key Principle**: Ask about specific past experiences, NOT hypotheticals.
- ✅ Good: "Tell me about the last time you felt unsafe walking somewhere."
- ❌ Bad: "Would you feel unsafe walking in X neighborhood?"

#### Core Questions:

5. **"Tell me about a time when you felt concerned about safety while walking or traveling somewhere."**
   - Follow-up: "What happened? How did you feel?"
   - Follow-up: "What did you do in that situation?"

6. **"How often do you think about safety when planning your routes or activities?"**
   - Daily? Weekly? Never?
   - Which situations trigger safety concerns?

7. **"Walk me through the last time you needed to figure out if an area was safe before going there."**
   - Where were you going? Why?
   - What did you do to assess safety?
   - How satisfied were you with the information you found?

8. **"What tools or resources do you currently use to check if an area is safe?"**
   - Websites? Apps? People?
   - How well do they work? What's missing?

9. **"Tell me about a time when you changed your plans or route because of safety concerns."**
   - What was the situation?
   - What information influenced your decision?
   - How did it affect your day?

10. **"If you could wave a magic wand and have perfect information about safety, what would that look like?"**
    - What would you want to know?
    - When would you want it?
    - How would you use it?

### Solution Discovery (3 minutes)

_Goal: Understand what they've already tried._

11. **"Have you ever used any apps or services to help with safety or crime information?"**
    - Which ones? When?
    - What did you like? What was frustrating?
    - Are you still using them? Why or why not?

12. **"If I told you I was building a tool to help with this problem, what would make you actually use it?"**
    - What features would be essential?
    - What would make it better than what you're doing now?

### Willingness to Pay (2 minutes)

_Goal: Gauge if this is a "nice to have" or "must have."_

13. **"How much time or money do you currently spend dealing with this problem?"**
    - Longer routes? Uber instead of walking? Research time?

14. **"If there was a service that solved this problem perfectly, would you pay for it?"**
    - If yes: "How much do you think would be reasonable?"
    - If no: "What would make it worth paying for?"

### Wrap-Up (1 minute)

15. **"Is there anything else about safety or this problem that I should know?"**

16. **"Do you know anyone else who might have similar concerns and would be willing to chat with me?"**
    - Get 2-3 referrals (snowball sampling)

> "Thank you so much for your time and insights. This has been incredibly helpful. If you'd like, I can keep you updated as I make progress on this project. Would that be okay?"

## Interview Best Practices

### Do's ✅

1. **Listen more than you talk** (80/20 rule)
2. **Ask about past behavior**, not future intentions
   - "Tell me about the last time..." NOT "Would you..."
3. **Dig deeper** with follow-up questions
   - "Tell me more about that."
   - "Why did you do that?"
   - "How did that make you feel?"
4. **Take notes** or record (with permission)
5. **Stay neutral** - don't lead or pitch
6. **Focus on the problem**, not your solution

### Don'ts ❌

1. **Don't pitch your solution** - This is discovery, not sales
2. **Don't ask leading questions**
   - Bad: "Don't you think it would be great if..."
3. **Don't ask hypotheticals**
   - Bad: "Would you use an app that..."
4. **Don't explain their problem to them**
5. **Don't ignore negative feedback**
6. **Don't cherry-pick quotes** that support your assumptions

## Success Criteria

### Problem Validation Checklist

We'll know the problem is real and worth solving if:

- [ ] **8 out of 10** interviewees confirm they have this problem
- [ ] **6 out of 10** say the problem is painful enough to actively seek solutions
- [ ] **5 out of 10** have tried multiple alternatives and remain unsatisfied
- [ ] **4 out of 10** express willingness to pay for a better solution
- [ ] **Specific stories** of when the problem caused real consequences

### Red Flags 🚩

Signs the problem might not be worth solving:

- Most people say "yeah, I guess" but can't give specific examples
- People don't currently do anything about the problem
- The pain is mild ("slightly annoying" vs. "keeps me up at night")
- No willingness to pay (free or nothing)
- Problem only exists in hypothetical scenarios
