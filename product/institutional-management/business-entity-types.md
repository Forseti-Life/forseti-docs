## FOR-PROFIT ENTITIES

### 1. Basic Structures (No Formal State Filing Required)

#### 1.1 Sole Proprietorship

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Single owner only
- No legal separation between owner and business
- Default structure for unregistered single-owner businesses
- Simplest form of business

**Liability**:
- Unlimited personal liability
- Owner personally responsible for all debts and legal claims

**Taxation**:
- Pass-through (Schedule C on personal tax return)
- Self-employment tax applies
- No separate business tax return

**Formation**:
- No state filing required
- May need local business license
- DBA ("Doing Business As") filing if using business name

**Best For**:
- Solo consultants or freelancers
- Very low-risk operations
- Testing business concept
- **Rare for institutions due to liability risk**

**Restrictions**:
- Cannot have employees (technically owner + employees = partnership)
- Cannot take on investors
- Difficult to sell or transfer

---

#### 1.2 General Partnership

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Two or more owners
- No formal filing required in most states
- Created by agreement (written or verbal) or by default when 2+ people operate business
- Each partner is an agent of the partnership

**Liability**:
- Unlimited personal liability for all partners
- Each partner liable for actions of other partners
- Joint and several liability (creditors can go after any partner for full amount)

**Taxation**:
- Pass-through taxation
- Form 1065 (partnership return) filed
- Each partner receives K-1 showing their share
- Partners pay self-employment tax on their share

**Formation**:
- Partnership agreement (recommended but not required)
- May need local business license
- EIN from IRS

**Best For**:
- Professional partnerships (doctors, lawyers)
- Family businesses (though LLC usually better)
- Short-term joint ventures
- **Rare for institutions due to liability concerns**

**Partner Types**:
- Active partners (manage business)
- Silent partners (invest but don't manage)
- All have equal liability unless specified otherwise

---

### 2. Limited Liability Structures (State Filing Required)

#### 2.1 Limited Partnership (LP)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Two types of partners: General Partners + Limited Partners
- Must have at least one general partner and one limited partner
- State filing required (Certificate of Limited Partnership)

**Partner Roles**:
- **General Partners**:
  - Manage the business
  - Unlimited personal liability
  - Can be individuals or entities
- **Limited Partners**:
  - Passive investors only
  - Limited liability (only lose investment)
  - Cannot participate in management (or lose limited liability protection)

**Liability**:
- General partners: Unlimited personal liability
- Limited partners: Liability limited to investment amount

**Taxation**:
- Pass-through taxation
- Form 1065 filed
- K-1s issued to all partners

**Formation**:
- File Certificate of Limited Partnership with state
- Partnership agreement required
- EIN from IRS
- Annual reports and fees

**Best For**:
- Real estate investments
- Family Limited Partnerships (estate planning)
- Private equity funds
- Institutional facilities with passive investors

**Cost**:
- Filing fees: $100-$500 depending on state
- Attorney fees: $1,000-$5,000

---

#### 2.2 Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- All partners have limited liability
- Variation of general partnership with liability protection
- Not available in all states
- Often restricted to licensed professionals

**Liability**:
- Partners not personally liable for partnership debts
- Partners not liable for malpractice of other partners
- Partners still liable for their own malpractice/negligence

**Taxation**:
- Pass-through taxation
- Form 1065 filed
- K-1s to partners

**Formation**:
- File registration with state
- Partnership agreement
- Often requires professional liability insurance
- Annual reports

**Best For**:
- Law firms
- Accounting firms
- Medical practices
- Architectural firms
- **Good for professional group practices (clinics, therapy centers)**

**Restrictions**:
- Many states limit to specific professions
- Must maintain professional liability insurance
- All partners typically must be licensed professionals

**Cost**:
- Filing fees: $100-$500
- Annual fees: $50-$300

---

#### 2.3 Limited Liability Company (LLC)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Hybrid structure (combines corporate liability protection with partnership taxation)
- Can be single-member or multi-member
- Owners called "members"
- Flexible management structure (member-managed or manager-managed)
- Most popular structure for small to medium businesses

**Liability**:
- Members have limited liability
- Personal assets protected from business debts/lawsuits
- Protection can be pierced if proper formalities not maintained

**Taxation Options**:
- **Default**:
  - Single-member: Disregarded entity (Schedule C)
  - Multi-member: Partnership (Form 1065)
- **Optional Elections**:
  - S-Corporation taxation (Form 2553)
  - C-Corporation taxation (Form 8832)

**Formation**:
- File Articles of Organization with state
- Operating Agreement (highly recommended, required in some states)
- EIN from IRS
- Annual reports and fees

**Best For**:
- Most small to medium institutions
- Child care centers
- Small schools
- Healthcare clinics
- Treatment centers
- **Most flexible and popular choice**

**Advantages**:
- Limited liability protection
- Pass-through taxation (default)
- Flexible profit distribution
- Less formality than corporations
- Can have single member
- Can elect different tax treatment

**Disadvantages**:
- Self-employment tax on all earnings (unless electing S-corp)
- Annual fees in most states
- Less established than corporations

**Cost**:
- Filing fees: $50-$500 depending on state
- Annual fees: $0-$800 (California highest)
- Attorney fees: $500-$2,000

---

#### 2.4 Professional Limited Liability Company (PLLC)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- LLC specifically for licensed professionals
- State-specific rules and requirements
- Similar to LLC but for professional services

**Requirements**:
- All members must be licensed in the profession
- Often called "PLLC" instead of "LLC"
- Some states require it; others allow regular LLC

**Professions Typically Required to Use PLLC**:
- Doctors, physicians, surgeons
- Dentists
- Lawyers, attorneys
- Accountants, CPAs
- Architects
- Engineers
- Veterinarians
- Therapists, counselors (in some states)
- Physical therapists
- Chiropractors

**Liability**:
- Protected from business debts
- Not protected from own professional malpractice
- Must carry professional liability insurance

**Taxation**:
- Same as LLC (pass-through default)
- Can elect S-corp or C-corp taxation

**Best For**:
- Medical clinics
- Dental practices
- Mental health practices
- Physical therapy centers
- **Any healthcare institution with licensed professionals**

**Cost**:
- Similar to LLC ($50-$500 filing)
- Often higher insurance requirements

### 3. Corporate Structures

#### 3.1 C-Corporation

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Separate legal entity from owners
- Owned by shareholders
- Governed by board of directors
- Managed by officers (CEO, CFO, etc.)
- Most formal business structure

**Ownership Structure**:
- **Shareholders**: Own the company through stock
- **Board of Directors**: Elected by shareholders, set policy
- **Officers**: Appointed by board, run day-to-day operations

**Liability**:
- Shareholders have limited liability
- Only investment at risk
- Corporate veil protection (if formalities maintained)

**Taxation**:
- **Double Taxation**:
  - Corporation pays tax on profits (21% federal)
  - Shareholders pay tax on dividends
- Files Form 1120 (corporate return)

**Formation**:
- File Articles of Incorporation with state
- Create corporate bylaws
- Issue stock certificates
- Hold organizational meeting
- Annual shareholder meetings required
- Keep corporate minutes

**Compliance Requirements**:
- Annual meetings (shareholders and directors)
- Meeting minutes must be kept
- Annual reports filed with state
- More complex accounting and record-keeping

**Best For**:
- Large institutions
- Multi-location operations
- Raising venture capital or investor funding
- Planning to go public eventually
- International operations

**Advantages**:
- Unlimited number of shareholders
- Can have multiple classes of stock
- Easier to raise capital
- Can issue stock options to employees
- Perpetual existence
- Strong liability protection

**Disadvantages**:
- Double taxation
- More complex and expensive to maintain
- Extensive formalities required
- More government regulation

**Cost**:
- Filing fees: $100-$800
- Annual fees: $50-$800
- Attorney/formation costs: $2,000-$10,000
- Ongoing compliance costs higher

---

#### 3.2 S-Corporation

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Not a separate entity type—it's a tax election
- C-Corporation or LLC elects S-corp tax status
- Provides pass-through taxation while maintaining corporate structure

**Eligibility Requirements** (Strict IRS Rules):
- Maximum 100 shareholders
- All shareholders must be:
  - US citizens or resident aliens
  - Individuals (not other corporations or partnerships)
  - Trusts or estates (with restrictions)
- Only one class of stock allowed (though different voting rights okay)
- Cannot be certain types of businesses (banks, insurance companies)

**Taxation**:
- Pass-through taxation (like partnership)
- Files Form 1120-S
- Issues K-1s to shareholders
- Shareholders pay tax on their share of profits
- Can pay reasonable salary (subject to payroll tax) + distributions (not subject to self-employment tax)

**Tax Advantage**:
- **Self-Employment Tax Savings**:
  - LLC: All profit subject to 15.3% self-employment tax
  - S-Corp: Only salary subject to payroll tax, distributions are not
  - Example: $100K profit
    - LLC: $15,300 self-employment tax
    - S-Corp: Pay $60K salary ($9,180 payroll tax), $40K distribution ($0 tax) = $9,180 total

**Formation**:
- Form corporation or LLC first
- File Form 2553 with IRS (S-corp election)
- Must file within specific timeframe (typically by March 15 of tax year)

**Compliance**:
- Must pay reasonable salary to working shareholders
- Payroll required (even for single owner)
- Form 1120-S filed annually
- More complex than LLC, less than C-corp

**Best For**:
- Profitable institutions with US owners
- Owners who want to minimize self-employment tax
- Small to medium institutions
- 1-5 owners typically

**Disadvantages**:
- Strict IRS requirements
- Ownership restrictions
- Must run payroll
- Cannot have different profit sharing arrangements
- Lose benefits if rules violated

**Cost**:
- S-corp election: Free (Form 2553)
- Payroll service: $40-$150/month
- Additional accounting costs: $500-$2,000/year

---

#### 3.3 Professional Corporation (PC)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Corporation specifically for licensed professionals
- Similar to C-corp but for professional services
- State-specific rules

**Requirements**:
- All shareholders must be licensed in the profession
- Often uses "PC" or "P.C." designation
- Subject to professional regulations

**Professions**:
- Same as PLLC (doctors, lawyers, accountants, architects, etc.)

**Liability**:
- Protected from business debts
- Not protected from own malpractice
- Sometimes limited protection from other professionals' malpractice

**Taxation**:
- Taxed as C-corp (default)
- Can elect S-corp taxation if eligible
- Double taxation if C-corp

**Best For**:
- Professional group practices
- Medical practices
- Law firms
- Accounting firms

**States That Require/Allow PC**:
- Requirements vary significantly by state
- Some states require PC, others allow PLLC, some allow both

**Cost**:
- Similar to C-corp ($100-$800 filing)
- Higher insurance requirements

---

### 4. Hybrid & Special Purpose Structures

#### 4.1 Benefit Corporation (B-Corp)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- For-profit corporation with social/environmental mission
- Legally required to consider impact on:
  - Shareholders
  - Employees
  - Community
  - Environment
- Different from B-Corp certification (separate process)

**Legal Obligations**:
- Must have general or specific public benefit purpose in articles
- Directors must consider stakeholder interests (not just shareholder profits)
- Annual benefit report required
- Third-party standard assessment (varies by state)

**Liability**:
- Same as C-corporation
- Shareholders have limited liability

**Taxation**:
- Taxed as C-corporation (double taxation)
- Can elect S-corp status if eligible
- No special tax benefits

**Formation**:
- File as Benefit Corporation (or amend existing corp)
- Articles must include benefit purpose
- Available in 38+ states and DC

**Reporting**:
- Annual benefit report (public)
- Must assess performance against third-party standard
- Higher transparency requirements

**Best For**:
- Mission-driven for-profit institutions
- Social enterprises
- Companies seeking impact investment
- Institutions balancing profit and social good

**Examples in Institutional Space**:
- Mission-driven schools
- Healthcare with focus on underserved
- Treatment centers with social mission

**Advantages**:
- Legal protection for mission-driven decisions
- Attracts impact investors
- May attract mission-aligned customers/employees
- Good PR/marketing

**Disadvantages**:
- Same double taxation as C-corp
- Additional reporting burden
- May be harder to attract traditional investors
- Directors face competing obligations

**Available States**:
- Most states (38+), including CA, NY, FL, TX, PA, IL, etc.
- Check specific state requirements

**Cost**:
- Similar to C-corp
- Additional cost for benefit report and assessment

---

#### 4.2 Public Benefit Corporation (PBC)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Similar to Benefit Corporation
- Specific public benefit must be stated
- More stringent than general B-Corp in some states

**Delaware PBC**:
- Most popular version
- Used by many tech companies
- Balance profit and purpose

**Differences from B-Corp**:
- B-Corp: General public benefit
- PBC: Specific public benefit must be stated
- Requirements vary by state (some states use terms interchangeably)

**Best For**:
- Same as Benefit Corporation
- Companies wanting to be more specific about their mission

---

#### 4.3 Low-Profit Limited Liability Company (L3C)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Hybrid between nonprofit and for-profit
- Social enterprise structure
- For-profit but mission-driven
- Facilitates program-related investments (PRIs) from foundations

**Requirements**:
- Primary purpose must be charitable or educational (like 501(c)(3))
- Profit-making must be secondary
- Cannot have political/lobbying as primary purpose

**Taxation**:
- Taxed as LLC (pass-through default)
- Can elect corporate taxation
- NOT tax-exempt

**Formation**:
- File as L3C with state
- Operating agreement required
- Only available in limited states

**Available States** (Very Limited):
- Vermont (first, 2008)
- Utah
- Louisiana
- Michigan
- Rhode Island
- Wyoming
- Illinois (repealed)
- North Carolina
- Oregon (repealed)
- **Not widely adopted**

**Best For**:
- Social enterprises
- Mission-driven institutions seeking foundation funding
- Hybrid nonprofit/for-profit models

**Challenges**:
- Limited state availability
- IRS hasn't provided clear guidance
- Foundations still cautious about PRIs to L3Cs
- Not widely understood

**Cost**:
- Similar to LLC

---

#### 4.4 Cooperative (Co-op)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Business owned and democratically controlled by members
- Members are customers, workers, or producers
- One member, one vote (typically)
- Profits distributed to members (patronage dividends)

**Types of Cooperatives**:

**Consumer Cooperative**:
- Owned by customers
- Examples: Food co-ops, credit unions, hardware stores

**Worker Cooperative**:
- Owned and controlled by employees
- Democratic workplace
- Workers share profits

**Producer Cooperative**:
- Owned by producers (farmers, craftspeople)
- Market their products collectively

**Housing Cooperative**:
- Members own shares giving right to occupy unit
- Common in NYC and other cities

**Formation Options**:
- Incorporated cooperative (files with state)
- Cooperative LLC
- Unincorporated association

**Taxation**:
- Cooperatives can deduct patronage dividends
- Single taxation (avoids double taxation)
- Members pay tax on distributions
- Files Form 1120-C or 1065 (if LLC)

**Governance**:
- Democratic control (one member, one vote)
- Elected board of directors
- Member meetings

**Best For**:
- Community-owned institutions
- Worker-owned businesses
- Member-focused organizations
- Child care co-ops (parent-owned)
- Preschool co-ops

**Principles** (Rochdale Principles):
1. Voluntary and open membership
2. Democratic member control
3. Member economic participation
4. Autonomy and independence
5. Education, training, information
6. Cooperation among cooperatives
7. Concern for community

**Advantages**:
- Democratic control
- Profits stay with members
- Community ownership
- Tax benefits

**Disadvantages**:
- Slower decision-making
- Hard to raise outside capital
- Less understood than traditional structures
- More complex governance

**Cost**:
- Varies widely by state and type
- $200-$2,000 formation costs

---

### 5. Public Market Structures

#### 5.1 Publicly Traded Corporation

**Legal Characteristics**:
- C-corporation with shares traded on stock exchange
- Subject to SEC regulations
- Extensive reporting and compliance requirements

**Requirements**:
- Must register with SEC
- File Form S-1 (registration statement) for IPO
- Ongoing reporting:
  - Form 10-K (annual report)
  - Form 10-Q (quarterly report)
  - Form 8-K (current events)
- Sarbanes-Oxley compliance
- Proxy statements

**Stock Exchanges**:
- NYSE (New York Stock Exchange)
- NASDAQ
- Must meet listing requirements

**Best For**:
- Very large institutions
- Multi-state or national chains
- Significant capital needs
- **Rare in institutional management space**

**Examples**:
- For-profit education companies
- Hospital corporations
- Correctional corporations (CCA, GEO Group)
- Child care chains

**Cost**:
- IPO costs: $1M-$10M+
- Annual compliance: $500K-$5M+
- Extensive legal and accounting

---

#### 5.2 Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Special tax status for real estate investors
- Corporation or trust that owns income-producing real estate
- Must distribute 90% of taxable income as dividends

**Requirements**:
- 75%+ of assets in real estate
- 75%+ of gross income from real estate
- Distribute 90% of taxable income
- 100+ shareholders
- No more than 50% owned by 5 or fewer individuals

**Taxation**:
- REIT pays no corporate tax (if distributes 90%)
- Shareholders pay tax on dividends
- Avoids double taxation

**Best For**:
- Real estate holding companies
- Institutional facilities focused on property ownership
- **Rare for operating institutions**

**Relevance to Institutions**:
- Could own institutional facility real estate
- Separate operating company from property ownership

## NONPROFIT ENTITIES

### Overview

Nonprofit entities are organized for purposes other than generating profit. They can generate revenue but must reinvest it in the mission (cannot distribute to owners/members). Many are tax-exempt under IRS Section 501(c).

---

### 1. 501(c)(3) Organizations - Charitable Organizations ⭐

**Most Common and Important Nonprofit Category**

#### 1.1 Public Charities

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Organized for charitable, religious, educational, scientific, or literary purposes
- Must receive broad public support (pass public support test)
- Tax-exempt under IRC Section 501(c)(3)
- Donations are tax-deductible to donors

**Qualifying Purposes**:
- **Religious** organizations (churches, synagogues, mosques)
- **Educational** organizations (schools, universities, tutoring programs)
- **Charitable** organizations (poverty relief, housing, disaster relief)
- **Scientific** research organizations
- **Literary** organizations
- **Testing for public safety**
- **Fostering national or international amateur sports** competition
- **Prevention of cruelty to children or animals**

**Public Support Test**:
- Must receive at least 1/3 of support from:
  - Government grants
  - Contributions from general public
  - Contributions/grants from other public charities
- OR pass facts and circumstances test

**Taxation**:
- Federal income tax exempt
- Must file Form 990 annually (unless very small)
- Donors can deduct contributions on their taxes

**Restrictions**:
- **No private inurement**: Cannot benefit private individuals
- **No political campaigning**: Cannot support/oppose candidates
- **Limited lobbying**: Can do some advocacy but restricted
- **Unrelated business income**: Taxed if substantial
- **Assets locked for charitable purposes**: Cannot be distributed to individuals

**Formation**:
- Incorporate as nonprofit corporation (state)
- File Form 1023 or 1023-EZ with IRS for tax exemption
- Must have articles of incorporation with:
  - Charitable purpose statement
  - Dissolution clause (assets go to another charity)
- Bylaws
- Board of directors (typically 3+ members)

**Governance**:
- Board of directors (volunteer, unpaid)
- Cannot be controlled by one person/family
- Must have independent board members
- Officers (may be paid reasonable compensation)

**Compliance**:
- Annual Form 990 (public document)
- State annual reports
- State fundraising registration (if soliciting donations)
- Maintain tax-exempt status

**Best For**:
- Mission-driven educational institutions (nonprofit schools)
- Charitable healthcare (free clinics, community health)
- Social service programs
- Religious institutions
- **Any institution focused on mission over profit**

**Sub-Categories**:

**Churches and Religious Organizations**:
- No Form 1023 required (automatic exemption)
- Don't have to file Form 990
- Special protections

**Schools, Colleges, Universities**:
- Must have regular curriculum
- Regular enrolled body of students
- Regular faculty
- Non-discriminatory policy required

**Hospitals and Medical Research**:
- Community benefit standard
- Must provide charity care
- Extensive reporting (Form 990 Schedule H)

**Public Safety Testing Organizations**:
- Test consumer products for safety

**Advantages**:
- Tax-exempt (federal and often state)
- Donors get tax deduction
- Grants from foundations and government
- Reduced postal rates
- Credibility and trust
- Property tax exemptions (often)

**Disadvantages**:
- No ownership/equity
- Cannot distribute profits
- Extensive regulations and reporting
- Public disclosure (Form 990)
- Limited political activity
- More difficult to get started

**Cost**:
- State incorporation: $50-$200
- Form 1023: $600 (or $275 for 1023-EZ)
- Attorney fees: $2,000-$10,000
- Annual compliance: $500-$5,000+

---

#### 1.2 Private Foundations

**Legal Characteristics**:
- 501(c)(3) organizations that don't qualify as public charities
- Typically funded by single family, individual, or corporation
- Must distribute 5% of assets annually

**Types**:

**Private Operating Foundation**:
- Operates own charitable programs
- Must spend 85% of adjusted net income on active programs

**Private Non-Operating Foundation**:
- Makes grants to other charities
- Doesn't operate programs directly

**Funding Sources**:
- Single donor or family
- Corporate funding
- Investment income
- Generally don't fundraise from public

**Taxation**:
- Tax-exempt
- 1-2% excise tax on investment income
- Stricter rules than public charities

**Restrictions**:
- Must distribute 5% of assets annually
- Prohibited from:
  - Self-dealing with donors/board
  - Excess business holdings
  - Jeopardizing investments
  - Taxable expenditures
- More extensive rules and penalties

**Governance**:
- Can be controlled by family
- Board can be family members
- Less independent than public charity

**Reporting**:
- Form 990-PF (public)
- More detailed than Form 990
- Investment listings public

**Best For**:
- Wealthy families wanting to do philanthropy
- Corporate foundations
- Grant-making organizations
- NOT for operating institutions

**Disadvantages**:
- 5% payout requirement
- Excise tax on investment income
- Stricter rules and penalties
- Public must be able to review returns

**Cost**:
- Formation similar to public charity
- Higher ongoing compliance costs

---

### 2. 501(c)(4) - Social Welfare Organizations

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Civic leagues, social welfare organizations
- Must promote social welfare of community
- Tax-exempt but donations NOT tax-deductible

**Qualifying Purposes**:
- Community improvement
- Civic betterment
- Social welfare
- Advocacy for community issues

**Key Difference from 501(c)(3)**:
- CAN engage in political activity (supporting/opposing candidates)
- BUT political activity cannot be primary purpose
- Must be 51%+ social welfare activities

**Taxation**:
- Tax-exempt
- Donations NOT tax-deductible
- Lobbying and political spending not tax-deductible

**Political Activity**:
- Can support/oppose candidates
- Can lobby extensively
- Popular for advocacy organizations
- "Dark money" groups often 501(c)(4)

**Formation**:
- Incorporate as nonprofit corporation
- File Form 1024-A with IRS
- Must notify IRS within 60 days of formation

**Reporting**:
- Form 990
- Don't have to disclose donors publicly

**Best For**:
- Community advocacy groups
- Homeowner associations
- Volunteer fire departments
- Advocacy-focused social services
- Political advocacy (secondary to social welfare)

**Examples Relevant to Institutions**:
- Community education advocacy groups
- Healthcare access advocacy
- Neighborhood improvement associations

**Disadvantages**:
- Donations not tax-deductible
- Political activity limits
- Less foundation grant eligibility

---

### 3. 501(c)(5) - Labor, Agricultural, Horticultural Organizations

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Labor unions, agricultural and horticultural organizations
- Must promote interests of members
- Tax-exempt

**Qualifying Organizations**:
- Labor unions
- Agricultural organizations (farmers, ranchers)
- Horticultural organizations
- Employee associations

**Purpose**:
- Better working conditions
- Better products
- More efficiency
- Improve grade of products

**Taxation**:
- Tax-exempt
- Donations NOT tax-deductible

**Political Activity**:
- Can engage in some political activity
- Can lobby

**Best For**:
- Labor unions
- Farm bureaus
- Agricultural cooperatives
- **Not typically relevant to institutional management**

---

### 4. 501(c)(6) - Business Leagues, Chambers of Commerce

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Business leagues, chambers of commerce, trade associations
- Promote common business interests
- Tax-exempt

**Qualifying Organizations**:
- Chambers of commerce
- Trade associations
- Professional associations
- Real estate boards
- Business leagues

**Purpose**:
- Improve business conditions in one or more lines of business
- Not for profit
- No private benefit

**Taxation**:
- Tax-exempt
- Donations NOT tax-deductible
- Member dues may be partially deductible as business expense

**Activities**:
- Industry advocacy and lobbying
- Professional development
- Networking
- Standard-setting

**Examples Relevant to Institutions**:
- **National Association of Child Care Professionals**
- **American Dental Association**
- **State School Associations**
- **Healthcare Industry Associations**

**Best For**:
- Industry trade groups
- Professional membership organizations
- Chambers of commerce

**Formation**:
- Incorporate as nonprofit
- File Form 1024
- Membership structure

---

### 5. 501(c)(7) - Social and Recreation Clubs

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Organized for pleasure, recreation, social purposes
- Supported substantially by membership
- No private benefit

**Qualifying Organizations**:
- Country clubs
- Dinner clubs
- Hobby clubs
- Golf clubs
- Swim clubs
- College fraternities/sororities

**Requirements**:
- Membership organization
- Substantially all activities for members
- Supported primarily by dues, fees, assessments

**Taxation**:
- Tax-exempt
- Investment income taxed
- Income from non-members taxed

**Restrictions**:
- Cannot discriminate based on race, color, religion (some)
- Limited non-member income allowed

**Best For**:
- Private clubs
- Member recreation organizations
- **Not typically relevant to institutional management**

---

### 6. 501(c)(8) - Fraternal Beneficiary Societies

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Fraternal organizations operating under lodge system
- Provide life, sick, accident insurance to members

**Requirements**:
- Lodge system (parent-local structure)
- Fraternal purposes
- Provide insurance benefits to members

**Examples**:
- Knights of Columbus
- Elks
- Moose
- Fraternal Order of Police

**Best For**:
- Fraternal organizations with insurance
- **Not relevant to institutional management**

---

### 7. 501(c)(9) - Voluntary Employee Beneficiary Associations (VEBA)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Provide life, sick, accident, or similar benefits to members or dependents

**Purpose**:
- Employee benefit plans
- Union benefit plans
- Similar benefit arrangements

**Best For**:
- Employee benefit trusts
- **Not relevant to institutional management**

---

### 8. 501(c)(10) - Domestic Fraternal Societies

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Fraternal organizations
- Lodge system
- Devote proceeds to charitable purposes

**Different from 501(c)(8)**:
- Don't provide insurance
- Focus on charitable purposes

**Best For**:
- Fraternal organizations
- **Not relevant to institutional management**

---

### 9. 501(c)(19) - Veterans Organizations

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Veterans posts or organizations
- 75%+ members must be veterans
- Substantially all other members must be veterans, cadets, or auxiliary

**Qualifying Organizations**:
- American Legion
- Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW)
- Disabled American Veterans (DAV)
- Vietnam Veterans of America

**Requirements**:
- Chartered by Congress or organized in US
- 75%+ veterans
- Operate for veterans' welfare

**Taxation**:
- Tax-exempt
- Donations ARE tax-deductible (unusual for non-501(c)(3))

**Best For**:
- Veterans organizations
- **Not relevant to institutional management**

### 10. Other 501(c) Categories

**501(c)(11)** - Teachers' Retirement Fund Associations

**501(c)(12)** - Benevolent Life Insurance Associations, Mutual Ditch/Irrigation Companies, Mutual/Cooperative Telephone Companies

**501(c)(13)** - Cemetery Companies

**501(c)(15)** - Mutual Insurance Companies

**501(c)(23)** - Veterans Organizations (pre-1880)

**501(c)(25)** - Title Holding Corporations for Multiple Parent Corporations

**501(c)(26)** - State-Sponsored High-Risk Health Coverage Organizations

**501(c)(27)** - State-Sponsored Workers' Compensation Reinsurance Organizations

**501(c)(29)** - CO-OP Health Insurance Issuers

**None of these are relevant to institutional management.**

### 11. Nonprofit Corporation (No Tax-Exempt Status)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Incorporated as nonprofit at state level
- Has NOT applied for or doesn't qualify for 501(c) status
- Not tax-exempt

**Why Exist**:
- Don't meet 501(c) requirements
- Don't want restrictions of tax-exempt status
- Small or informal
- Mutual benefit corporations

**Taxation**:
- Pay corporate income tax
- Donations NOT tax-deductible

**Formation**:
- Articles of incorporation (nonprofit)
- Bylaws
- No IRS filing

**Best For**:
- Homeowner associations
- Social clubs not seeking 501(c)(7)
- Mutual benefit organizations
- **Rare for institutions**

---

### 12. Nonprofit LLC

**Legal Characteristics**:
- LLC organized for nonprofit purposes
- Allowed in some states
- Can apply for 501(c) status

**Differences from Nonprofit Corporation**:
- LLC structure (more flexible)
- Can have members or be member-less
- Operating agreement instead of bylaws

**IRS Stance**:
- IRS will grant 501(c) status to LLCs
- Must meet all 501(c) requirements
- Less common than nonprofit corporations

**Best For**:
- Situations wanting LLC flexibility with nonprofit mission
- Single-member subsidiary of 501(c)(3)
- **Rare**

---

### 13. Unincorporated Nonprofit Association

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Group of people organized for nonprofit purpose
- No formal filing with state
- Common law entity

**Formation**:
- No filing required
- May have bylaws
- Informal structure

**Liability**:
- Varies by state
- Some states provide limited liability (Uniform Unincorporated Nonprofit Association Act)
- Historically, members had personal liability

**Best For**:
- Informal clubs
- Very small organizations
- **Not recommended for institutions** (liability risk)

---

### 14. Charitable Trust

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Not a corporation—it's a trust
- Property held for charitable purposes
- Managed by trustees

**Formation**:
- Trust document (declaration of trust)
- Names trustees
- States charitable purpose
- Can apply for 501(c)(3) status

**Governance**:
- Trustees (not directors)
- Follow trust document
- Fiduciary duties

**Best For**:
- Endowments
- Charitable gift planning
- **Not typically for operating institutions**

**Differences from Nonprofit Corporation**:
- Trust structure
- Trustees vs. directors
- Less formality
- Often used for passive charitable giving

## FAMILY & ESTATE PLANNING STRUCTURES

### 1. Family Limited Partnership (FLP)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Limited Partnership owned by family members
- Typically parents are general partners, children are limited partners
- Used for estate planning and asset protection

**Structure**:
- **General Partners**: Parents (1-2%), control management
- **Limited Partners**: Children/heirs (98-99%), passive ownership

**Purpose**:
- Transfer wealth to next generation
- Reduce estate taxes (valuation discounts)
- Asset protection
- Maintain control while transferring ownership

**Taxation**:
- Partnership taxation (Form 1065)
- Annual gifting of limited partnership interests
- Valuation discounts (lack of control, lack of marketability)

**Use for Institutions**:
- **Family-owned institutional businesses**
- Transfer to next generation while maintaining control
- Estate tax planning for wealthy families

**Formation**:
- File LP certificate
- Partnership agreement
- Annual meetings
- Proper formalities essential (IRS scrutiny)

**Requirements**:
- Must have legitimate business purpose (not just tax avoidance)
- Proper valuation
- Maintain formalities
- Arm's length transactions

**Advantages**:
- Estate tax savings
- Asset protection (limited partners)
- Centralized management
- Creditor protection

**Disadvantages**:
- IRS scrutiny
- Complex to maintain
- Professional advisors required
- Annual appraisals

**Cost**:
- Attorney fees: $5,000-$15,000
- Annual appraisals: $2,000-$10,000
- Ongoing accounting: $2,000-$5,000/year

---

### 2. Family LLC

**Legal Characteristics**:
- LLC owned by family members
- Similar purposes as FLP
- More flexible structure

**Structure**:
- **Manager-Managed**: Parents are managers
- **Members**: Parents and children

**Advantages over FLP**:
- More flexible
- Less formality
- Can allocate profits differently
- State law may be more favorable

**Use for Institutions**:
- Family-owned institutions
- Succession planning
- Estate planning

**Formation**:
- Form LLC
- Operating agreement
- Gift LLC interests to family members

---

### 3. Spousal Joint Venture

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Special classification for married couples
- Both spouses materially participate in business
- Each spouse files own Schedule C (instead of partnership return)

**Requirements (IRS)**:
- Must be married filing jointly
- Both spouses must materially participate
- Only business owned by spouses (no other owners)
- Not a corporation or partnership
- No election to be taxed as corporation

**Advantages**:
- Avoids partnership return (Form 1065)
- Both spouses get Social Security credits
- Simpler tax reporting
- Both spouses covered for self-employment tax purposes

**Best For**:
- Husband-wife owned institutions
- Both spouses actively work in business
- Want simplicity

**How to Report**:
- Each spouse files Schedule C
- Report 50/50 income and expenses (or based on actual participation)

---

### 4. Spousal Partnership

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Partnership between married couple
- Can be general partnership, LP, or LLC taxed as partnership

**Difference from Spousal Joint Venture**:
- Files Form 1065 (partnership return)
- More formal
- Can have unequal profit sharing

**Best For**:
- When spouses want different ownership percentages
- More complex arrangements
- When joint venture doesn't apply

---

### 5. Community Property Considerations

**Legal Characteristics**:
- In community property states, assets acquired during marriage are jointly owned 50/50
- Affects business ownership and taxation

**Community Property States** (9):
- Arizona
- California
- Idaho
- Louisiana
- Nevada
- New Mexico
- Texas
- Washington
- Wisconsin

**Impact on Businesses**:
- Business started during marriage may be community property
- Both spouses may have ownership rights even if only one spouse operates
- Affects divorce, death, creditor claims
- Tax elections available

**Qualified Joint Venture in Community Property States**:
- Can elect not to be treated as partnership
- Each spouse reports 50% on Schedule C

---

### 6. Family Trust as Business Owner

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Revocable or irrevocable trust owns business interests
- Used for estate planning
- Trustee manages assets for beneficiaries

**Types**:

**Revocable Living Trust**:
- Grantor maintains control
- Can be changed/revoked
- No tax benefits during life
- Avoids probate at death

**Irrevocable Trust**:
- Cannot be changed
- Assets removed from estate
- Estate tax savings
- Creditor protection

**Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT)**:
- Transfer business interests with reduced gift tax
- Grantor receives annuity payments
- Remainder to beneficiaries
- Popular for appreciating businesses

**Use for Institutions**:
- Estate planning for family-owned institutions
- Succession planning
- Asset protection

**Taxation**:
- Depends on trust type
- Grantor trusts: Taxed to grantor
- Complex trusts: Trust pays tax or passes through

---

### 7. Tenancy in Common

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Co-ownership by multiple individuals
- Each owns undivided interest (e.g., 25%, 50%)
- Can be unequal shares
- Each can sell their interest

**Use for Institutions**:
- Multiple unrelated owners
- Real estate ownership
- Flexible ownership arrangements

**Disadvantages**:
- No liability protection
- Difficult to manage
- **Better to use LLC or partnership**

---

### 8. Joint Tenancy with Right of Survivorship (JTWROS)

**Legal Characteristics**:
- Co-ownership with survivorship rights
- Equal ownership required
- When one owner dies, interest passes to surviving owner(s)

**Use**:
- Husband-wife ownership (simple)
- Avoid probate
- Equal partners

**Disadvantages**:
- No liability protection
- Must be equal ownership
- **LLC or partnership usually better**

## ENTITY SELECTION GUIDE

### For Institutions: Decision Tree

**Step 1: For-Profit or Nonprofit Mission?**

**NONPROFIT (Mission-Driven, No Owners)**
- → **501(c)(3) Public Charity**
  - Educational mission, healthcare mission, social services
  - Want tax exemption and donor tax deductions
  - Grants and donations primary revenue
  - ✅ **Best for most mission-driven institutions**

**FOR-PROFIT (Owners, Profit Distribution)**
- → Continue to Step 2

---

**Step 2: How many owners?**

**SINGLE OWNER**
- Small, low-risk → Sole Proprietorship (⚠️ high liability)
- **Most cases** → **Single-Member LLC** ✅

**TWO OWNERS (Married Couple)**
- Both work equally → **Spousal Joint Venture** or **LLC**
- Want simplicity → **LLC** ✅
- Estate planning needs → **Family LLC** ✅

**MULTIPLE OWNERS (Family)**
- Current operations → **LLC** ✅
- Estate planning focus → **Family Limited Partnership** or **Family LLC** ✅

**MULTIPLE OWNERS (Unrelated)**
- Small-medium → **LLC** ✅
- Planning to raise capital → **C-Corporation** ✅
- Want pass-through tax + corp structure → **LLC electing S-Corp** ✅

---

**Step 3: Special Considerations**

**Licensed Professionals (doctors, lawyers, therapists)**
- → **PLLC** or **Professional Corporation** ✅

**Want S-Corp Tax Benefits** (self-employment tax savings)
- Profitable, all US owners → **LLC electing S-Corp** or **S-Corporation** ✅

**Raising Venture Capital**
- → **C-Corporation** ✅

**Social Mission + For-Profit**
- → **Benefit Corporation** or **L3C** (limited states) ✅

**Community-Owned**
- → **Cooperative** ✅

---

### For-Profit Institution: Recommended Path

**Year 1 (Startup):**
- Form **LLC** (single-member or multi-member)
- Simple, flexible, liability protection
- Pass-through taxation
- Cost: ~$1,000

**Year 2-3 (Profitable):**
- Consider **S-Corp election** to save self-employment tax
- Requires payroll
- Cost: Add ~$2,000/year

**Year 5+ (Growing/Scaling):**
- If raising capital → **Convert to C-Corp**
- If multi-location → May stay LLC or convert to C-Corp
- If family succession → Consider **Family LLC/FLP**

---

### Nonprofit Institution: Recommended Path

**Year 1 (Formation):**
- Incorporate as **Nonprofit Corporation** (state)
- Apply for **501(c)(3)** status (IRS)
- Cost: ~$3,000-$5,000
- Timeline: 3-12 months for IRS approval

**Ongoing:**
- File Form 990 annually
- Maintain board governance
- Stay compliant with 501(c)(3) rules
